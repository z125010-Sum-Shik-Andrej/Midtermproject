Quantum entanglement describes a striking feature of quantum mechanics where two or more particles become linked in such a way that the state of one particle instantaneously influences the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are. When entangled, measurements on one particle immediately determine the outcome for its partner, reflecting a deep nonlocal correlation.

First highlighted by Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen, and later experimentally confirmed through Bell test experiments, entanglement defies classical intuition and underpins emerging quantum technologies. This "spooky action at a distance" enables protocols such as quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and device-independent quantum cryptography.

Today's research pushes entanglement into larger many-body systems, exploring its role in quantum simulation, error-corrected computing, and entanglement-enhanced sensing.
